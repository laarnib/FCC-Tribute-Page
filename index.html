Ariel Alesna Llanto

Death To White

Ariel Llanto

While planting fragmentary poems
    and doodling sketches on blank,
    vestal pieces of paper
    like a bee
    vigorously sprinkling freckles of pollen
    upon the cheeks of flowers,
    i had a peculiar feeling
    that anything marked by the color white
    never stays the same.

In nature
    evidence abounds of this:
    as fair clouds
    time and again turn gray
    at the weight of rain,
    as the sun
    roasts Caucasian skin,
    as dust
    eventually populates on pristine fluorescent lamps
    and yellow
    conquers the pages of novels.

White,
    the apex of clarity and cleanliness,
    cannot but be a prey of change;
    it is defenseless
    against the visibility
    of wet paint, food stains, or footprints
    unlike the darker shades.

A pity
    this fleeting color is subject
    to repeated demise
    for all its purity;
    change awaits it
    like a cursor on a vacant computer document
    blinking lustily
    for the next letter.
